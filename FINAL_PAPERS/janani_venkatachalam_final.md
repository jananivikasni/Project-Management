# IlliniRSO  

### Introduction: 
IlliniRSO is a centralized platform designed for all parties within the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign registered student organizations (RSOs). The intended purpose is to simplify the way in which students discover and join RSOs, communicate between members and officers, receive location and time updates for events, and navigate the overall experiences related to campus RSOs. The platform combines functions that are currently spread across multiple apps into a singular interface that acts as a central space of communication between officers, possible members, and pre-existing members. The main purpose is to reduce fragmentation of information, improve organization, and ultimately increase campus involvement in RSOs. My inspiration for this project comes from a combination of my personal experience attempting to join RSOs my freshman year as well as a collective understanding of inefficiencies in RSO communication systems by the students at this university. 
#### _Current Problem Space_  
As mentioned prior, the current environment in which University RSOs operate has greatly fragmented communication (Arnold, 2023). Students rely on various social media platforms to gather information on and communicate with RSOs. This causes scattered information due to the lack of a centralized and consistent communication channel. Among other negatives, students may miss important updates and event information, struggle to track organization involvement, and become overwhelmed by the number of information sources they must check regularly (Venkatachalam et al., 2025). Communication methods also vary heavily between organizations, creating inconsistent workflows and making engagement more difficult to maintain for both students and officers.
The absence of a centralized platform for RSOs additionally creates barriers to discovering and joining organizations. While it is generally easier to discover RSOs during Quad Day and other beginning-of-semester recruitment events, it becomes significantly more difficult to casually explore organizations later in the semester or academic year. By concentrating the majority of RSO promotion within a limited timeframe, the possible member pool becomes narrowed to students who are able to access that information during that window. Students who miss these opportunities due to time conflicts, lack of awareness, or other obstacles may remain unaware of organizations that align with their interests, majors, or career goals. Fragmented communication systems also create issues for RSO officers, as managing announcements across multiple platforms becomes repetitive and inefficient, maintaining engagement becomes more difficult, and inactive members become harder to reach (Venkatachalam et al., 2025). 
#### _Community (UIUC) Relevance & Personal Motivations_ 
On all college campuses, a majority of new students are experiencing a completely new environment in which they need to rebuild their community and learn how to sustain themselves emotionally without the support of those from their home life. RSOs extend beyond just the activities done in them; through these organizations, students are able to build friendships, develop soft and hard skills, find career opportunities, and explore personal interests. Registered student organizations are one of the easiest ways that students get involved on their campus and create new communities for themselves in an unfamiliar environment. By creating an interface in which students can become more easily involved, universities can foster stronger campus communities and improve student engagement and belonging (Haines, 2019).
This project is also heavily motivated by personal experiences during my freshman year at UIUC. As a new student, I initially want to wait and better understand the difficulty and time commitment to my coursework before becoming heavily involved in RSOs. However, after the beginning-of-semester recruitment period passes, it becomes significantly more difficult to find information about organizations that align with my interests. Different RSOs rely on entirely different communication platforms, requiring students to repeatedly download new applications, search through social media accounts, join separate messaging systems, and constantly update themselves across multiple disconnected platforms. This makes the process of joining and maintaining involvement far more time-consuming and inaccessible than necessary. Additionally, as someone with a strong interest in accessibility and human-centered design, I become increasingly interested in how a more centralized and organized system can reduce these barriers and create a more inclusive experience for students with different technological familiarity, communication preferences, schedules, and accessibility needs. 

### Project Scope, Workflow, & Information Organization 
#### _Existing Systems Analysis_ 
Current platforms that exist with similar capabilities to the features on IlliniRSO are as follows: Discord, GroupMe, OneIllinois, Campus Groups, Instagram, Facebook, and Slack (Venkatachalam et al., 2025). Platforms like Discord, GroupMe, and Slack allow for fast communication, easy announcements, and community interaction; however, they allow for minimal interaction and engagement by interested students and non-members. These platforms also do not have many features relating to information sharing other than instant message communication, like live maps, calendars, and more, unless explicitly provided through a link. On the other hand, OneIllinois and Campus Groups are university-sanctioned organization directories; however, most of the information on these sites is outdated or has links that lead to unavailable emails and websites. This is a result of the lack of addressing more than the one user need of a list and description of the RSOs on campus. 
#### _Scope_ 
_Setting Boundaries:_ The scope of IlliniRSO focuses on improving how UIUC students discover, join, and engage with Registered Student Organizations through a centralized platform. The project primarily centers on features such as organization discovery, messaging, announcements, event calendars, maps/navigation, and personalized recommendations. The scope is limited to workflow design, user research, and prototype development rather than a fully coded or university-deployed application. 
_Defining Deliverables:_ Key deliverables include the IlliniRSO prototype, workflow maps, interview analysis, accessibility recommendations, feature planning, testing plans, and project documentation. 
_Establishing Objectives:_ The main objectives of the project are to reduce fragmented communication between RSOs, improve accessibility and organization discoverability, simplify communication and event management, and strengthen student engagement and campus belonging. 
_Identifying Constraints & Assumptions:_ Constraints include limited development time, limited technical resources, prototype-level implementation, and a testing population primarily consisting of UIUC students. The project also assumes that students prefer centralized communication systems and that improving accessibility and organization will increase RSO participation and engagement. 
_Limitations:_ The scope user group is specifically targeted at University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign students, meaning the initial version and creation of this application may not include advanced analytics, monetization, or external integrations. 
#### _Agile Methodology_ 
This project uses iterative development for both the development and the design teams; as the project evolves over time, development occurs in cycles based on user feedback, user adoption, and use of specific features. Continuous revisions are made based on these metrics, and in earlier stages of development, revisions are made based on user testing, examples being: heuristic evaluations, alpha testing, and beta testing. The agile-style workflow allows for better adaptation, more flexible project goals, and features and prevents teams from being disconnected from each other due to its iterative nature.

### Documentation, Feedback, & Issue Managment 
#### _Feedback_ 
Feedback plays a major role throughout the development of IlliniRSO because the overall purpose of the platform is to improve the real experiences students and RSO officers currently have when navigating organization involvement at UIUC. Since the project is heavily centered around usability and accessibility, it is important for development decisions to be guided directly by user testing and student feedback rather than assumptions from the development team alone. Testing the platform with actual students helps identify workflow inefficiencies, confusing navigation patterns, communication issues, and accessibility concerns that may not be obvious during the design phase. User behavior and interaction patterns can be evaluated through surveys, usability testing sessions, interviews, feature requests, and bug reports. These methods help the team better understand how students naturally search for organizations, manage schedules, communicate with groups, and respond to notifications or event updates. 
#### _Issue Management_ 
Issue management is also necessary throughout development in order to keep the platform organized and functional. As technical problems or usability concerns are identified, bug tracking systems help prioritize fixes based on severity and impact on the user experience. Feature prioritization is also important because there are many possible additions to IlliniRSO, but not every feature realistically fits within the initial scope of development. The team needs to balance user needs with available development time and resources while focusing primarily on the most impactful and necessary workflows first. Communication between contributors additionally requires organized update logs, announcements, and documentation to ensure all teams remain aligned throughout development. 
#### _Documentation_ 
Documentation itself serves as one of the foundations of long-term maintainability for the project. Workflow maps, feature documentation, testing notes, design guidelines, and update logs help contributors understand how systems function, why design decisions are made, and how future improvements should be approached. This also reduces confusion between design, management, and technical teams during iterative development cycles. 
#### _Usability Improvements_ 
Usability improvements remain a continuous focus throughout the project. The platform prioritizes simple navigation, intuitive workflows, and clear information architecture in order to minimize confusion and reduce the amount of effort required for students to engage with RSOs. Additional customization features such as notification settings, personalized recommendations, and calendar integration further improves accessibility and creates a more user-centered experience overall. 

### Project Sustainability & Technical Growth 
#### _Long-Term Sustainability_ 
Long-term sustainability is extremely relevant to this project, as digital systems require continuous maintenance and while a majority of the information that changes in the application is changed by specific RSO officers, the IlliniRSO app must do things like add and remove RSOs based on current information. On top of that, students' needs are likely to evolve over time so maintaining the relevance of IlliniRSO also means making the application sustainable. Ongoing maintenance includes actions like updating features, fixing bugs, improving performance, and updating organizational information (only pertaining to the existence of RSOs, not how they function; that is up to the RSO officers).

#### _Technical Growth & Scalability_ 
As IlliniRSO continues to develop, scalability becomes one of the most important technical considerations. Since the platform is intended to centralize communication and Involving potentially hundreds of organizations and thousands of students, the system needs to support increasing numbers of users, organizations, events, and interactions without major performance issues. As engagement grows, the platform also needs to handle larger amounts of activity while maintaining fast navigation, organized communication, and consistent workflows. Future additions to the platform can include recommendation algorithms, advanced event tracking, AI-powered organization suggestions, analytics dashboards, and broader campus-wide integrations with university systems.
The success of this technical growth can be measured through scalability metrics such as the platform’s ability to support increased user traffic without performance loss. Additional success measurements can include feature adoption rates, engagement metrics related to calendars and messaging systems, workflow efficiency improvements, and user acquisition rates reflecting increases in student users and participating RSOs each semester. 
#### _Technical Debt_ 
Technical debt also becomes an important consideration throughout development. In many projects, rushed development decisions or poorly organized systems create future maintenance problems that become increasingly difficult to fix later. For IlliniRSO, this can include inefficient code, outdated workflows, or systems that make future feature expansion difficult. Since the project is intended to evolve over time, maintaining organized workflows and documentation is necessary to prevent these issues from growing as the platform expands.
Preventing technical debt requires iterative testing, long-term planning, and detailed documentation throughout development. Success can be measured through maintainability metrics, such as the ease of updating or expanding features, reductions in recurring bugs over time, and development efficiency when implementing updates. Documentation coverage and testing coverage also helps evaluate whether workflows and features are being properly maintained before deployment. 
#### _Automation_ 
Automation can additionally improve the efficiency and consistency of the platform. Possible automated systems can include event reminders, push notifications, calendar syncing, recommendation systems, and automated event promotion. These systems reduce repetitive work for RSO officers while helping students stay updated on organization activities and deadlines more consistently.
The success of these systems can be evaluated through notification effectiveness, engagement rates, operational efficiency, task completion rates, and automation accuracy. Metrics such as increases in RSVP activity, event attendance, and organization interaction help determine whether automation features are effectively improving engagement and communication. 
#### _Partnerships & Expansion_ 
Long-term expansion of IlliniRSO likely depends on partnerships with organizations such as Student Engagement, SODA, UIUC departments, and student organizations themselves (Student Organization Development and Administration, n.d.). These partnerships can increase adoption, strengthen feedback systems, and provide long-term support for the platform. Success can be measured through stakeholder adoption rates, partnership growth, feedback participation rates, community engagement metrics, and overall expansion of supported organizations, events, and campus integrations over time. 

### Conclusion 
Overall, IlliniRSO was designed as a centralized platform intended to simplify how students discover, join, and engage with Registered Student Organizations at UIUC. The project primarily focused on addressing the fragmented communication systems currently used across campus by combining organization discovery, communication, event management, and engagement tools into one interface. In doing so, the platform would improve workflows for both students and RSO officers while reducing the confusion and inefficiencies created by scattered information across multiple applications and communication channels.
Throughout the project, many concepts from integrative project management were applied, particularly coordinating workflows between different teams, balancing user needs with technical feasibility, and emphasizing planning and iterative development throughout the design process. The project also connected heavily to ideas surrounding community-oriented technology, as the platform was designed not only as a functional tool but also as a system that could strengthen student interaction, accessibility, and campus engagement overall.
More broadly, IlliniRSO demonstrates how thoughtful and user-centered technology design can improve campus involvement by making organizations easier to discover, communication more organized, and participation more accessible. The project ultimately reinforced the importance of accessibility, iterative development, sustainability, and long-term project planning when designing systems intended to support real communities and evolving user needs at UIUC.
