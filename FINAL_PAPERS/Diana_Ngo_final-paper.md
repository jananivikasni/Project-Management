### Abstract:
The H.E.R.O (Higher Educational Research Observatory) project responds to the concern of data collection methods embedded within privacy policies amongst higher education institutions. With the internet being a central part of people’s daily activity, long-winded privacy policies become difficult to interpret. As a result, online users have limited knowledge of how their data is being collected and used.
H.E.R.O aims to act as the bridge that connects users with complex privacy policies, providing a usable filtration system across 250+ U.S. institutions using data scraped from archived data. H.E.R.O provides an LLM-powered chatbot that encourages users to ask about detailed privacy policy changes, which otherwise take hours to detect.
The H.E.R.O project is designed for students and researchers seeking a quicker, more efficient way to understand privacy policies. Throughout the 16-week project timeline, UX/UI and backend teams work collaboratively, fostering project management principles of triaging strategies, documentation, public meetings, version control, and working openly.

### Introduction (Vision Statement):
In the age where the majority of education, resources, work, and entertainment reside online, people are now much more susceptible to privacy breaches, data leaks, and ultimately fall victim to the over-collecting of data. The Higher Educational Research Observatory aims to create greater accessibility and transparency to the data collection practices online that fly under the radar by collecting data from various university sites and their privacy policies. This paper will address the overall scope of the project, the tech stack, and possible technical debt, and the principles required to create a usable and scalable product.

### Big Picture & Details: 
H.E.R.O, otherwise known as the “Higher Educational Research Observatory” (H.E.R.O) is an interface that allows for a simple search of privacy policy changes through an LLMs Chatbot and internet-archived data regarding privacy policies across 250+ U.S institutions. The H.E.R.O project solves the issue with neglected privacy policy changes, which causes users to be aware of how their information is shared and collected. This desktop application serves the general public, students, and researchers who would like to search Internet archives through a more usable interface. I care about this project because privacy, especially in the emerging digital age, causes people to unknowingly distribute their information for corporations to take advantage of. The unethical data collection blinds our generation through long privacy policies and dark patterns. The primary community focus is students and researchers, in order to help students become more aware of how their information is being collected and help researchers discover patterns in online policy changes. In order to develop this project, tools such as Figma and Figma Make AI will be used to conceptualize the user interface. This project also requires research on LLMs & AI tools with constant communication with the project lead & backend team.

### Open Canvas: 
The H.E.R.O Open Canvas addresses the following issues:
- Users lack awareness of privacy policy changes on online websites
- Large volumes of personal information are collected without users' knowledge
- Inaccessible privacy policies and dark patterns prevent true understanding and consent

H.E.R.O's solution towards these privacy issues is to generate greater awareness so that everyone can especially track how privacy policies have changed to inform their choices when accepting website terms. This project aims to help students and researchers easily search, understand, and compare privacy policy changes across 250+ U.S. institutions using an LLM-powered interface and archived data. Utilizing AI to analyze the textual context of privacy policies, this creates a quicker, more efficient method for users to identify what information is being collected about them. The main target audience is undergraduate and postgraduate students, as well as researchers who are interested in data collection and technology. To extend user reach for the project, the project will be presented in academic-based settings, such as in classrooms, lectures, and research showcases.
Moreover, to successfully execute the H.E.R.O project, the team should include a balance of 2-3 UX / UI designers who are proficient in design software such as Figma, 4 back-end developers who have experience working with AI and LLMs, and 1-2 project leads who can manage logistical aspects such as IRB approval, conference submissions, and application launch. The team will need to create or seek a data scraper to collect privacy policies for all 251 U.S. educational institutions, create an AI / LLM to detect privacy changes, and a simplistic front-end design software. To effectively find qualified developers for the H.E.R.O project, recruitment may derive from research showcases, college classrooms relating to design or computer science, or advertising the project as an internship opportunity for students.

### Project Scope + WorkFlow:
Furthermore, the H.E.R.O project will span 16 weeks (3 and ¾ of a month), with the projected completion date in June 2026. The H.E.R.O project incorporates the collaboration of four different primary sectors from Product Management, UX/UI Design, Front End, and Back End. 
To review the overall workflow, the H.E.R.O project starts at week 1-2 with obtaining IRB approval, which is required by the ethics committee to authorize a research study involving human subjects. Once IRB approval is received, the H.E.R.O project can be initiated with consent to conduct research that can involve user feedback, interviews, and surveys. After approval, the UX / UI design team can start ideating and creating an information architecture to gain a basic understanding and purpose for the project. 
In week 3-4, the H.E.R.O project will then need to contact the UX/UI Design team to conduct a pain point audit and competitor analysis on the Labor Action Tracker website (https://striketracker.ilr.cornell.edu/). The pain point audit should include the positives and negatives of the website and how this website design can apply to the H.E.R.O user interface and information architecture. Once the foundational information architecture is created, UI components can be planned for the front end to prepare for the low-fidelity design. 
Within week 5, the low-fidelity design should be created in order to prepare for a user feedback session in week 6, followed by feedback implementation into the low-fidelity design in week 7. If the design is signed off by the project lead to present to the backend team, then the high-fidelity design process will be initiated with constant communication with the backend team to ensure feasibility and transparency. In the case that the project lead does not approve of the low-fidelity design, then the feedback must be implemented into the design until approval. Once the high-fidelity design is completed, this marks the first milestone of a completed design user interface.
Weeks 10 - 12 require recruitment for backend programmers who are more literate in AI automation and LLMs, so that both the UX/UI teams and the backend team can collaborate to build the final prototype and project demo. The project will then go through a week-long process of rapid user interviews and feedback sessions in week 13. One potential issue within the user feedback session is the recruitment for interviews, as there are limited voluntary team members, and the project isn’t feasible to outsource a user research team for one week of user interviews. Understanding this constraint, convenience sampling would be the most optimal, with user interviews being conducted by the UX / UI team and surveys being distributed nationwide through social media portals such as LinkedIn.

Once user interviews and surveys are closed, the front-end code will be pushed and connected to the backend in week 14, accomplishing a functional beta version of the H.E.R.O project. 
Within the final weeks, week 15-16 will focus on executing the AI integration through formalizing access to Anthropic or OpenAI API, and if able to gain an API token, the backend will be able to implement the API integration, completing with the AI privacy change detector. In week 16, the UX / UI team must create a Design Study Poster that encapsulates all the design and user interface of the project to then be submitted to the AIST&T and to prepare for the final launch. 

### Principles 1, 2, 3:
Throughout the process of the H.E.R.O. project, the following design principles are equipped to ensure focus on the project vision statement and ethical standards:

#### 1) Triaging Strategies (prioritize issues under severe time constraints)

The H.E.R.O Project’s timeline is within a 16-week time window (equivalent to 3 and ¾ months); as a result, strategic triaging strategies must be equipped in order to fuel consistent workflow and complete final deliverables. Triaging strategies extend beyond a traditional to-do list as this method accounts for the feasibility and determines prioritization of the tasks, resulting in greater productivity. For the H.E.R.O project, I’ve equipped the Eisenhower Matrix, which is a task-management tool to help identify tasks based on urgency. The Eisenhower Matrix includes four distinct quadrants: Do, Decide, Delegate, and Delete. The first row marks important tasks, and the second row marks unimportant tasks, while the first column represents urgent tasks, and the second column represents non-urgent tasks.

The H.E.R.O Project has multiple tasks that operate at various levels of importance and involvement across different teams. For example, in the top left quadrant (Do), tasks such as planning the design pitch, fixing a bug in the Figma prototype demo, and creating a master document outline the core features of the website are all urgent and important tasks that act as prerequisites to initiate the handoff from designers to backend. 
Within the top right quadrant (Decide), these tasks are not as urgent but remain essential to the completion of the project. The decision to either use the WayBack Machine to scrape universities’ privacy policy information or create our own database of scraped privacy policies is a decision that requires stakeholders, designers, and the backend team to discuss the advantages and disadvantages. However, this database decision is crucial to the operation of the website, but not necessarily urgent, as tasks such as the UI/UX and workflow bug fixes take priority. 
The third quadrant, located in the bottom left corner (Delegate), represents tasks that are urgent but not as important; these are tasks that can be delegated to other team members with either greater expertise or the ability to complete with efficiency. In the Delegate section, we must have a backend engineer who is well-versed in AI technology to attend the hand-off meetings regarding website expectations and user flows of the LLM-powered privacy change detector and live AI chatbot. Other tasks, such as logo design, don’t affect the website’s flow and functionality, but are urgent because there needs to be a quality, clean design to represent the H.E.R.O project when submitting to the AIST&T conference. 
Lastly, within the fourth quadrant on the bottom right-hand corner (Delete), are the tasks that are both not urgent and not important, which typically include tasks that are meant to eliminate certain features, unnecessary tasks, workflows, and items that somewhat hinder the efficiency and speed of the project. 

#### 2) Documentation and Public Meetings
Furthermore, the H.E.R.O project operates as a cross-collaborative effort spanning Product Management, UX/UI Design, Front End, and Back End. As a result, structured documentation practices help reduce the significant risk of miscommunication, time consumption of repetitive workflows, and design inconsistencies, especially as the project approaches the final launch and submission date and scales in future iterations. 
To minimize these risks, the H.E.R.O project will centralize all communication and documentation across the following platforms: 
Google Drive – for creating meeting agendas, document AI engineering prompts, research documentation, and data on privacy policies. 
Outlook – for holding Teams meetings, planning calendar events, and cross-communication between design and backend teams 

The H.E.R.O project will conduct weekly scheduled check-ins across all teams. There will be virtual one-on-ones between the UX designers and the project lead, along with a one-on-one meeting between the backend engineers and the project lead. Additionally, a second meeting will be held on a bi-weekly basis with all teams, including the project lead, the UX designers, and the backend. This consistent meeting structure will encourage constant communication with the project lead’s vision and pass on specific weekly information and tasks simultaneously to the design and backend teams. All meeting notes and agendas will be located in Google Drive respective folders within a master Google Drive Folder that is only accessible and editable by developers of the H.E.R.O project. This agenda should be created by the host of the meeting, typically the project lead, incorporating urgent/important needs, action items, and important dates in the project scope. 
This aggressive documentation is critical during the handoff stages between the design team and backend regarding constructing design flows that represent the targeted user demographic and the core intent of the H.E.R.O project. Through maintaining consistent, open documentation and forms of communication, the H.E.R.O project will be able to ensure coordination across all teams. 


#### 2) Version-control/Project Awareness & Working Open
Although the H.E.R.O project is initially a short-term initiative, there is potential for scalability after submission to the AIST&T. Therefore, implementing working open practices and a version-control system will prepare the H.E.R.O project for greater scalability and contributions. Version control unites all teams to intentionally coordinate UX/UI, front-end, and backend systems along a structured timeline. 
To manage code and track updates, GitHub will be used to track developments. Back-end teams work fully remote and are from varying time zones, so maintaining a collaborative space where changes and updates are clearly communicated is crucial. All developers will work on their individual branches, working on the LLM-powered chatpoint and database integration, utilizing tools such as Co-pilot and ClaudeCode for programming. For all merges a pull request will be required to cross-check each other's code before pushing to the main branch. 
From the UX/UI and frontend perspectives, version control will be maintained in Figma, with a set library of styles to maintain visual consistency and a master document with all components and variants. Having a set design library during mid-fidelity and high-fidelity wireframing not only ensures consistency but also helps when colors, text, or sizes need to be adjusted throughout all screens. Utilizing Figma as the primary design software weighs benefits in ensuring safe saving, history, individualized contributions, and collaborative design as everyone can edit at once.
Moreover, by documenting design initiation and development in GitHub repositories and Figma, the team will be able to apply their individual skills to H.E.R.O’s primary purpose as an application. Practicing transparency throughout the project avoids information being centralized to one group or individual, but across all teams. Project awareness, especially between smaller teams such as H.E.R.O’s, is pivotal to stay on the directed timeline. Besides GitHub and Figma, a shared Google Folder and document will be created to track more holistic weekly goals that apply to backend, UX/UI, and frontend teams. For each one-on-one meeting and all-teams meeting, the Google document will be referred to at the start of the meeting to summarize completed tasks from the previous week, roadblocks and potential issues, upcoming tasks, and goals. 
As for working openly, currently the H.E.R.O project isn’t publicized online for external contributors as a way to protect the H.E.R.O idea before submission to the ASIS&T. According to working open practices recommended “How to Work Open” by OpenMatt in Mozilla, the team should work openly if they have the proper tools in place to meaningfully absorb that public participation. At this point, H.E.R.O is still forming its initial idea and team; therefore, working openly wouldn’t be as beneficial yet, but will be in the future. Working openly has great potential to contribute useful participation, and without working openly, the development process grows slowly. After submitting to the ASIS&T, the H.E.R.O project could utilize Etherpads and community lists to generate large transparency in the continued development process. By allowing external contributors to view and provide feedback after ASIS&T submission, the H.E.R.O project has a solid foundation with a purpose, product demo, and research to ignite the conversation towards improvement. As a result, H.E.R.O would gain credibility, more inclusive perspectives, and faster debugging processes.


### Technical Stack:
The H.E.R.O projects utilize a technical stack that includes tools and sources to support team collaboration to warrant a seamless onboarding flow for recruited engineers and designers, and scalability for multiple iterative designs. For UX/UI Design, tools such as Figma and Figma Make AI are used to create wireframes, prototypes, and consistent design libraries and components. For backend development, Claude Code will act as an AI assistant to support developers in converting designs into a functional interface. For the collection of privacy policy data for the 250+ U.S. educational institutions, the data will be derived from Internet Archive and the Wayback Machine, using scraping tools such as Beautiful Soup to extract and process the data. For version control and cross-collaboration between all teams, GitHub will be used for code management and updates. 

### Technical Debt & Potential Debt:
Although the H.E.R.O project aims to utilize a strong technical stack to foster clear documentation, collaboration, and scalability, each decision presents its individual challenges toward potential technical debt in the long term. 

__Figma & Figma Make:__         
Figma is a foundational tool for designing wireframes, components, and creating visual prototypes. Figma Make is Figma’s AI design tool that helps conceptualize ideas, test design alternatives, and rapidly iterate on design changes. 
However, there are certain risks with utilizing both Figma Make and Figma. Within a short-term project, an over-reliance on AI design generated content from Figma may present a disconnect in creating an interface that fully represents user needs and maintains usability. Design could be generated in Figma Make and copied to a Figma file dashboard to create the design manually, but with the current version of Figma Make, prototyping animations and screen connections can not be transferred over to Figma dashboards. As a result, the utilization of both Figma and Figma Make risks unnecessary time expenditure on editing and fixing Figma Make outputs, when that time could be utilized to focus on user testing and designing for usability. When scaling the H.E.R.O project with larger teams, the time spent on fixing AI outputs would not be efficient in the long-term design process. Overall, the technical debt is relatively low to middle level, as Figma allows for customization and Figma Make, although not perfect, the platform presents great advantages in quicker A/B testing time and design changes.  

__Claude Code:__   
Similarly, backend development using Claude Code presents a similar issue, but at a higher risk, as the backend development primarily relies on Claude Code. The choice to code using Claude Code presents apparent issues such as outputting less readable code, debugging difficulties, and system maintenance. However, using Claude Code allows for fast development, allowing for a beta version of the product to be submitted to ASIS&T. The primary system is acknowledged as only a short-term method, with the benefit of creating a fully functional system quickly. On the downside, code may be less understood, which risks confusion between the backend team when debugging. Additionally, when considering the long-term impact, as the backend team grows, the developers may become dependent on the AI-generated outputs or need to completely redo the code to be clearer.  Overall, the technical debt is high and not feasible for a long-term time frame. In order to compensate, after submission and approval from the ASIS&T, discussions on next steps will be necessary so the H.E.R.O’s code isn’t fully reliant on Claude AI. 

__Internet Archive, Wayback Machine, Beautiful Soup:__ 
Scraping data for privacy policies across various platforms fosters reliability, especially when constructing the H.E.R.O LLM chatbot modal. However, there is a risk with inconsistent formats and missing data that would require constant updates and upkeep. As a result, the technical debt is moderate to high, the data website scrapers are at risk of breaking long-term, requiring maintenance, but it is an accessible way to collect historical data, and it works well for the initial beta product. 


### Conclusion      

Overall, the H.E.R.O (Higher Educational Research Observatory) aims to create greater transparency regarding privacy policy changes across universities’ websites. With the growing concerns about digital privacy, H.E.R.O focuses on providing students and researchers with a usable, accessible interface to understand these privacy policy changes over time. 
Interconnecting UX/UI design tools for developing front-end experience, Claude AI for backend development, scraping archived website data, and utilizing GitHub for version control systems, H.E.R.O., as a short-term project, can complete a cohesive product to be ultimately submitted to ASIS&T at the end of the 16-week timeline. Acknowledging the various tradeoffs with long-term scaling, H.E.R.O would require focus on reconstructing systems and processes within the backend code to increase clarity, to quicken debugging, and optimize workflow for product updates. 
From a product management perspective, the fundamental practices of triaging strategies, documentation/public meetings, version-control, and working with open/project awareness, the H.E.R.O product takes intentional steps to drive active, clear engagement between teams. Ultimately, H.E.R.O attempts to return agency to online users, allowing them to understand and decipher how their information is being collected without needing to surf through abundant textual context seen in privacy policies.
